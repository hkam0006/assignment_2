Silent Threat in the Air

Air pollution is an invisible menace that affects the lives of millions worldwide. From the dense smog of urban areas to the less conspicuous pollutants in our atmosphere, the air we breathe can have profound consequences on our health and the climate.

Global Impact of Air Pollution on Mortality

Air pollution, resulting from the presence of outdoor and indoor particulate matter, along with ozone, constitutes a significant risk factor for several major causes of mortality, encompassing heart disease, stroke, lower respiratory infections, lung cancer, diabetes, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

In 2019, air pollution contributed to 11.53% of global fatalities, establishing it as the second most prevalent cause of death, surpassed only by deaths attributed to cardiovascular diseases.

In 1990, global fatalities attributed to air pollution accounted for 13% of the total. Over the course of three decades, there was only a minimal 2% reduction in the fatality rate, signaling a slow pace of improvement.

Deaths attributed to Air Pollution per 10,000

This graph depicts a significant and encouraging trend in air pollution-related deaths per 10,000 people from the year 1990 to 2019. Over this 29-year period, we observe a steady decrease in air pollution-related deaths globally.

Solomon Islands maintains the highest number of deaths attributed to air pollution per 10,000 people throughout this period. In 2019, Solomon Islands recorded a distressing 432.93 deaths per 10,000 due to air pollution.

Air Pollution and Climate

Air pollutants not only severely impact public health, but also the earth's climate and ecoysstem globally. Many sources of outdoor air pollution are also sources of high CO2 emissions. For example, the use of fossil fules for power generation, industry and polluting transport are all major sources of both particulate matter and CO2. In terms of CO2 emissions per capita, Qatar, the UAE, and Saudi Arabia lead the world, with Qatar leading the pack at a per capita CO2 emission of 36 tonnes. This trend can be primarily attributed to their significant reliance on oil production and consumption.

The Impact of Air Pollution on Rising Temperatures

Due to the presence of air pollutants, including CO2 and other greenhouse gases, the global temperature has been steadily rising compared to the average temperature recorded between 1901 and 2000.

Yearly land and ocean temperature compared to 1901-2000 average. Blue bars indicate cooler than average years, red bars shows warmer than average years. Hover over chart to see details.

The escalating global average temperatures are exacerbating the frequency and severity of natural disasters. This rise in temperature contributes to the intensification of weather-related events such as hurricanes, wildfires, and droughts, making them more destructive and challenging to mitigate.

Flooding has emerged as the predominant form of natural disaster, with its frequency on the rise due to the melting of ice caps, leading to higher ocean water levels. The incidence of floods in 2019 surged by about 19 times in comparison to 1950.
