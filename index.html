Silent Threat in the Air

Air pollution is an invisible menace that affects the lives of millions worldwide. From the dense smog of urban areas to the less conspicuous pollutants in our atmosphere, the air we breathe can have profound consequences on our health and the climate.

World Air Pollution

This map visualizes the Air Quality Index (AQI) values for countries worldwide, providing insights into air quality and health risks. The United States, with an alarming AQI of 321.50, leads in air pollution. South Africa follows with an AQI of 165, while India reports 161. These figures emphasize the global need for air quality management and environmental efforts, underscoring the urgency to address air pollution's impact on health and the environment.

Deaths attributed to Air Pollution

Air pollution, resulting from the presence of outdoor and indoor particulate matter, along with ozone, constitutes a significant risk factor for several major causes of mortality, encompassing heart disease, stroke, lower respiratory infections, lung cancer, diabetes, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).

In 2019, air pollution contributed to 11.53% of global fatalities, establishing it as the second most prevalent cause of death, surpassed only by deaths attributed to cardiovascular diseases.

In 1990, global fatalities accounted for 13% of the total. Over the course of three decades, there was only a minimal 2% reduction in the fatality rate, signaling a slow pace of improvement.

Air Pollution and Climate

Air pollutants not only severely impact public health, but also the earth's climate and ecoysstem globally. Many sources of outdoor air pollution are also sources of high CO2 emissions. For example, the use of fossil fules for power generation, industry and polluting transport are all major sources of both particulate matter and CO2.

Global Temperature

As a result of air pollution such as CO2 and other greenhouse gases, the global temperature relative to 1901-2000 average temperature is trending upwards.
